Understand Payments

A payment is an international banking term that refers to a directive to a bank or other financial institution from a bank account holder instructing the bank to make a payment or series of payments to a third party.

Payments in Backbase Platform allow the user to transfer money from one account to another. Users either select the account to transfer to from their contact list, their own accounts, or they specify new account data. Users also have the option to save new account data to their contact list.

Users can schedule payments for one-time execution or recur during a defined period or for a defined number of times. Administrators control whether standing orders are possible with a preference on the widget. Users can also make batch payments by uploading a file.

Backbase Platform always works in conjunction with a core banking system for the actual processing of a transaction. The widget that initiates the payment sends the payment order to the backend, which is either Backbase’s own DBS or a third-party system. The payment order ends up in the core banking system that processes the payment order and, if successful, sends the payment to the beneficiary.

Initiate SEPA payment

Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA) is an effort of the European Union to harmonize euro payments between banks. A credit transfer is an electronic payment from one bank account to another. The SEPA Credit Transfer (SCT) scheme makes the transfer of money in Europe easy and convenient (source: European Payments Council).

Users initiate a SEPA credit transfer from their account to any other SEPA reachable account.

Initiate UK payment

For UK customers, supported payment types include the following:

  • Faster Payments: Suited for smaller transactions with a £250k maximum. The paying and the receiving banks must be direct or indirect members of the service. Payments are processed almost instantly or at least within two hours. Banks usually charge no fee. Read more on the Faster Payments website.

  • CHAPS: Clearing House Automated Payment System, or CHAPS, is used to make payments that need to be processed on the same working day. Because the transfer fee that banks charge for a CHAPS payment is relatively high, Faster Payments is the payment system used for most payments and CHAPS is used for high-value transactions above £250k.

Initiate US payment

The United States have several electronic payment methods, such as a US domestic wire transfer and ACH payments.

Business Banking covers the following payment methods:

  • US domestic wire transfer: Payment from a US account to another US account (at a different financial institution)

  • ACH debit transfer: Incoming money transfer (or direct debit) from a US account to another US account

  • ACH credit transfer: Outgoing money transfer from a US account to another US account

About wire transfers: Wire transfers are processed individually, in contrast to Automated Clearing House (ACH) payments that are processed in batches. The individual processing means that wire transfers are usually processed faster than ACH payments. Wire transfers are either domestic or foreign and adhere to different schemes. One main difference is that US domestic wire transfers require the ABA routing number of the beneficiary’s bank, while US foreign wire transfers require the receiving bank' SWIFT code.

Read more about the Key Differences Between ACH and Wire Transfers, or other money transfer methods: How to Send Money Online.

About ACH payments: ACH payments are electronic payments from a US account to another US account. The ACH operator (Automated Clearing House operator) acts as an intermediary between the financial institutions of the beneficiary and the sender of the payment.

With ACH payments the following terms apply:

  • The sender is called the originator.

  • The financial institution from the originator is called the Originating Depository Financial Institution (originating DFI).

  • The counterparty is called the receiver.

  • The financial institution of the receiver is called the Receiving Depository Financial Institution (receiving DFI).

Financial institutions process ACH payments in batches, meaning the funds are not transferred in real-time, but within one or two business days.

Read more about ACH payments: What Does ACH Stand For?

About ACH debit: ACH debit is also known as direct payment (in the US) or direct debit (in other markets). Its most common use is in the recurring billing set up between a customer and a biller, such as a utility company, insurance company, or lender. An ACH debit transaction occurs when the originator of a transaction authorizes the recipient to pull funds from their account.

The following example outlines what happens when a customer wants to pay an electric bill via ACH debit:

  1. The recipient requests that the customer pays their bill. In this example, the recipient is the electric company.

  2. The customer authorizes their ODFI to send money from their account to the recipient’s account.

  3. The RDFI uses the customer’s routing and account numbers to send a request through a clearing house such as the Federal Reserve.

  4. The clearing house contacts the ODFI to request the transfer of funds.

  5. Because the ODFI has already received authorization from the customer, it pulls the funds from the customer’s account to the recipient’s account on the pre-determined date.

  6. If the customer has enabled recurring billing, this process repeats at the specified interval. Enabling recurring billing authorizes the recipient to regularly withdraw funds from the customer’s account.

About ACH credit: ACH credit is also known as direct deposit. A common use is in the recurring payment of wages by an employer to an employee.

ACH payments use a Standard Entry Class code (SEC code) to identify different aspects of the payment, such as debit or credit, or consumer or corporate. With DBS, users set the SEC code to:

  • PPD: Prearranged payment and deposits, which is meant for payments to or from consumers.

  • CCD: Corporate credit or debit entry, which is meant for payments to or from companies.

  • CIE: Consumer entry for credit applications, which is meant for payments to a company for funds owed to that company.

For more information, see List of SEC codes.

Initiate international transfer

International transfers are payments to another country.

Because the required payment details differ per country, the widget requests the expected payment details from the backend and flexibly adjusts the input controls and validations based on the response.

For example, the customer wants to make a payment from a US account to an EU account. The widget requests the expected payment details from the backend. The backend returns that the allowed currencies are US Dollar and Euro and the expected account number format is IBAN. The widget uses this information to assemble the next screen where the customer enters the necessary payment details and creates the payment.

Intracompany payment orders

Intracompany payment orders are payment orders where both the originator and counterparty accounts belong to the same legal entity. They are also known as intra-legal entity payment orders. Multiple payment types are supported out of the box.

When the user navigates to the review screen and the payment order is validated, the user is informed if it qualifies as an intracompany payment order.

Custom payment types can also be used as intracompany payment types. After creating and adding the custom payment type, it can be configured in the payment type mapping configuration.

An intracompany payment order is validated using the same rules as its regular counterpart. Any custom validators that apply to the regular type automatically apply to the intracompany type as well.

Manage payment orders

The initiate payment functionalities support scheduling a payment to be executed in the future, either as a one-off or as a standing order. The manage payment orders functionality lists these future payment orders and allows users to manage them.

  • Search payment orders.

  • Approve or reject an individual payment order (available when Approvals is enabled).

  • Approve or reject several payment orders at the same time, and view a notification if the approval failed for at least one item.

  • Cancel a payment order.

  • Print the details of a payment order.

Payment templates

A payment template is a copy of an existing payment order that speeds up creating similar payment orders in the future.

The Initiate omni payment widget supports storing a successfully completed payment as a template from the payment confirmation view. When a bank customer initiates an omni payment, they may use a template from a list of templates associated with the currently initiated payment type. Moreover, when selecting a template to create a new payment, the user is only able to view templates they are allowed to create a payment from, this is determined by the entitlements of the user. After selecting a template, the widget pre-fills the payment form with the data saved in the template. The bank customer is able to overwrite any pre-filled data.
